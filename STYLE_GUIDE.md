# Fashion Analysis Style Guide

A comprehensive reference for evaluating clothing and outfits using design principles, color science, and fashion standards.

---

## 1. COLOR SCIENCE & THEORY

### Color Relationships

**Complementary Colors**
- Opposite on the color wheel
- Create high contrast, vibrant combinations
- Example: Orange + Blue, Red + Green, Yellow + Purple
- Trend-Forward: Embraces boldly complementary combinations
- Heritage Minimalist: Uses subdued complementary pairs for sophistication

**Analogous Colors**
- Adjacent on the color wheel (30-60° apart)
- Harmonious, pleasing to the eye
- Example: Blue + Blue-Green + Green
- Both personas appreciate this harmony; Minimalist finds it more versatile

**Triadic Colors**
- Three colors equally spaced on the color wheel (120° apart)
- Vibrant but balanced
- Example: Red + Yellow + Blue
- Trend-Forward: Loves the energy; Minimalist: Cautions against being busy

**Monochromatic**
- One color in varying tints/shades/tones
- Elegant, cohesive, sophisticated
- Example: Light gray + medium gray + dark gray + charcoal
- Heritage Minimalist: Gold standard for timeless sophistication
- Trend-Forward: Can feel boring without intentional texture contrast

**Achromatic**
- Black, white, and gray only
- Ultimate neutrality and timelessness
- Heritage Minimalist: Perfect wardrobe foundation
- Trend-Forward: Sees as backdrop for colorful statement pieces

### Color Psychology & Associations

**Warm Colors** (Red, Orange, Yellow)
- Psychology: Energy, warmth, passion, creativity
- Flatters: Warm undertones, deep skin
- Effect: Advances visually, draws attention, energizing

**Cool Colors** (Blue, Purple, Green)
- Psychology: Calm, trust, sophistication, growth
- Flatters: Cool undertones, light skin
- Effect: Recedes visually, soothing, refined

**Neutral Colors** (Black, White, Gray, Beige, Navy, Camel)
- Psychology: Balanced, sophisticated, timeless
- Versatility: Pairs with any color
- Heritage Minimalist: Foundation of every wardrobe
- Trend-Forward: Canvas for bold pieces

### Undertone Analysis

**Warm Undertones**
- Yellow, gold, peachy base
- Flatters: Warm colors (gold, olive, warm greens, warm reds)
- Examples: Cream, warm beige, warm white, warm gray
- Best neutrals: Camel, warm beige, warm bronze

**Cool Undertones**
- Blue, pink, ashy base
- Flatters: Cool colors (silver, jewel tones, icy colors)
- Examples: Icy white, cool beige, ash gray, platinum
- Best neutrals: Cool gray, cool white, navy, cool camel

**Neutral Undertones** (Rare)
- Can wear both warm and cool effectively
- Advantage: Maximum versatility

### Color Saturation & Value

**High Saturation**
- Vivid, bold, pure colors
- More noticeable, makes statement
- Requires confidence or context
- Examples: Bright red, saturated sage, vivid blue

**Low Saturation** (Muted)
- Grayed-down, toned versions of colors
- More sophisticated, easier to wear
- Versatile, timeless
- Examples: Dusty rose, muted sage, slate blue

**Brightness Value**
- Light: High lightness (pastels, pale tones)
- Medium: Mid-range brightness (most colors)
- Dark: Low lightness (navy, black, deep burgundy)
- Balance: Avoid all-light or all-dark outfits; create visual contrast

## 2. DESIGN PRINCIPLES

### Rule of Thirds

**Visual Grid**
- Divide space into 9 equal sections (3×3 grid)
- Focal points placed on intersections or lines create dynamic composition
- Application to fashion: Outfit focal point should be at 1/3 from top or bottom, not centered

**In Outfit Styling:**
- Visual focal point at 1/3 height (belt, statement necklace, color block)
- Creates more dynamic, intentional look than centering everything
- Heritage Minimalist: Uses for subtle sophistication
- Trend-Forward: Emphasizes with bold accent placement

### Balance

**Symmetrical Balance**
- Equal weight on both sides
- Formal, structured, stable
- Can feel static or boring if not intentional
- Heritage Minimalist: Appreciates order and predictability

**Asymmetrical Balance**
- Unequal visual weight distributed evenly
- More dynamic, interesting, modern
- Requires intentional design
- Trend-Forward: Loves the inherent movement

**Radial Balance**
- Elements radiating from center point
- Less common in fashion; seen in patterns or circular accessories
- Creates focal point naturally

### Proportion & Scale

**Golden Ratio** (1.618:1)
- Appears naturally pleasing to the human eye
- Example: Waist at 1/3 down from shoulders (classic silhouette)
- Sleeve length: Wrist point at roughly golden ratio from shoulder

**Body Proportions Balance**
- **Fit Balance:** Fitted top + loose bottom = balanced (or vice versa)
  - Never all fitted (constraining, overwhelming)
  - Never all loose (shapeless, drowning)
  
- **Visual Weight Balance:**
  - Heavy visual weight (dark, busy, textured): Best at smaller area
  - Light visual weight (bright, plain, smooth): Can be larger area
  - Example: Lightweight blouse (large) + dark jeans (small) = balanced

- **Length Balance:**
  - Cropped top: Works with high-waisted or full-length bottom (creates proportion play)
  - Long top: Works with fitted bottom to avoid overwhelming frame
  - Oversized length: Needs slim or tapered bottom to define silhouette

### Emphasis & Focal Point

**Creating Focal Point:**
1. **Contrast**: Place opposite color where you want attention
2. **Texture**: Busy texture draws eye more than plain
3. **Shape**: Unusual silhouette becomes natural focal point
4. **Scale**: Large element in sea of small elements (and vice versa)

**Strategic Focal Points:**
- Face/neckline (necklace, scarf, collar detail)
- Waist (belt, contrasting color, seaming)
- Chest (color block, pattern, texture)
- Proportion breaks (cropped, tapered, flared)

**Hierarchy:**
- One PRIMARY focal point (main attention)
- Optional secondary focal point (supporting interest)
- Never multiple equal focal points (creates visual chaos)

### Movement & Flow

**Line Direction:**
- **Horizontal lines**: Widening, stable, grounding (good for top-heavy frames)
- **Vertical lines**: Lengthening, slimming, sophisticated (draws eye up-down)
- **Diagonal lines**: Dynamic, energetic, modern (stripes, color blocks, draping)
- **Curved lines**: Soft, feminine, flowing (rounded necklines, curved hems)

**Visual Flow:**
- Eyes should follow intentional path through outfit
- Avoid abrupt visual "dead zones"
- Use color, texture, or seaming to guide eye naturally

### Pattern & Texture

**Pattern Scale Compatibility:**
- Small frame: Small-scale patterns work (tiny floral, fine stripes)
- Large frame: Large-scale patterns prevent overwhelming
- Mixed patterns: Must differ significantly in scale (avoid two medium patterns together)

**Pattern Mixing Rules:**
1. **Limit to 2-3 patterns maximum** (beyond that = chaos)
2. **Vary scale significantly** (small + large, not small + medium)
3. **Share color bridge** (one color appears in both patterns)
4. **Maintain formality alignment** (professional florals + professional stripes okay; casual plaid + casual gingham okay)

**Texture Considerations:**
- **Visual weight**: Rough texture (linen, cable knit, tweed) feels heavier than smooth
- **Fabric movement**: Flowy fabrics (silk, chiffon) drape; structured fabrics (cotton, linen) hold shape
- **Mixing textures**: Combine different textures for visual interest
  - Smooth + rough = contrast
  - Matte + shiny = interest
  - Avoid: All mattte (flat) or all shiny (overwhelming)

## 3. FASHION-SPECIFIC PRINCIPLES

### Silhouette Harmony

**Silhouette Types:**
1. **Straight** - Hangs vertically, no curves, same width throughout
   - Flatters pear/apple shapes
   - Modern, androgynous, versatile
   
2. **Fitted** - Closely follows body curves, hugs shape
   - Flatters hourglass shapes
   - Requires good fit; misfit is obvious
   
3. **A-Line** - Fitted at top, flares toward bottom
   - Flatters pear/larger hip shapes
   - Romantic, flattering, balanced
   
4. **Oversized** - Intentionally larger than body
   - Requires intentional styling (belt, layering, cropping)
   - Modern, casual, can hide frame or overwhelm
   
5. **Tapered** - Fitted at top, narrows at bottom
   - Flatters heavier top/proportions
   - Contemporary, requires proper proportion

**Silhouette Pairing:**
- Never all fitted (claustrophobic, reveals every flaw)
- Never all loose (shapeless, overwhelming)
- Best practice: One fitted + one looser piece = balanced
  - Example: Fitted top + straight/loose pants
  - Example: Oversized sweater + fitted leggings

### Visual Weight Distribution

**What Creates Visual Weight:**
1. **Dark Colors** - Black, navy, burgundy = heavy
2. **Busy Patterns** - Multiple colors/patterns = heavy
3. **Thick Fabrics** - Wool, leather, corduroy = heavy
4. **Texture** - Rough/nubby fabrics = heavy
5. **Embellishments** - Studs, sequins, ruffles = heavy
6. **Volume** - Oversized, puffy = heavy

**What Lightens:**
1. **Bright/Light Colors** - White, cream, pale colors = light
2. **Plain Fabrics** - Solids = light
3. **Thin Fabrics** - Linen, chiffon, silk = light
4. **Smooth Texture** = light
5. **Simple Design** = light
6. **Fitted Cut** = light

**Balance Principle:**
- Heavy visual weight concentrated in small area = balanced
- Light visual weight spread across large area = balanced
- Heavy weight spread large OR light concentrated small = unbalanced

**Examples:**
- ✅ Small dark blazer + large light blouse = balanced
- ✅ Dark jeans (small area) + light oversized sweater = balanced
- ❌ Light small top + large dark pants = unbalanced (weight crashes to bottom)

### Fit Principles

**Sleeve Length:**
- **Perfect fit**: Hits at wrist bone (half-inch above wrist)
- **Too long**: Overwhelms frame, creates bulk
- **Too short**: Shortens arm line, awkward proportions
- Roll-up sleeves: Intentional styling, adds modern touch

**Shoulder Seam:**
- **Perfect fit**: Sits at shoulder point (where shoulder naturally ends)
- **Too wide**: Overwhelms frame, creates unflattering line
- **Too narrow**: Restricts movement, looks wrong
- Critical for overall proportion perception

**Waist Placement:**
- **Sitting waist** (natural waist, smallest part): Most flattering for most shapes
- **High waist** (above natural waist): Lengthens leg, flatters some shapes, formal
- **Low waist** (below natural waist): Creates bulk, shortens leg, casual
- Consistency: High-waisted bottom pairs best with tucked/cropped top

**Length Proportions:**
- **Shirt length**: Hits mid-hip (not at widest hip point)
- **Pants inseam**: Just touches top of shoe, shows ankle bone
- **Dress/skirt length**: Knee (most versatile), mid-calf (elongates), midi (trendy), mini (youthful)
- Proportions relative to frame: Tall = can wear any; Petite = shorter lengths better

### Formality Levels

**Casual** (Everyday wear)
- Fabric: Cotton, denim, linen, basic knits
- Silhouette: Straight, relaxed, comfortable
- Details: Minimal; practical seaming
- Color: Any (often brighter, more personality)
- Examples: T-shirt + jeans, cotton dress, knit sweater

**Business Casual** (Office/creative professional)
- Fabric: Cotton blends, wool, silk blends
- Silhouette: Tailored but not stiff; fitted or slightly relaxed
- Details: Quality seaming, professional finishes
- Color: Mostly neutrals with accent colors acceptable
- Examples: Blazer + chinos, professional dress, tailored pants + blouse

**Business** (Corporate/formal office)
- Fabric: Wool, silk, high-quality blends
- Silhouette: Tailored, structured, clean
- Details: Professional finishes, quality construction
- Color: Predominantly neutral (black, navy, gray, white)
- Examples: Blazer + dress pants, suit, professional dress

**Formal** (Evening/special occasions)
- Fabric: Luxury materials (silk, satin, tulle, velvet)
- Silhouette: Elegant, intentional, refined
- Details: Exceptional finishes, luxury seaming
- Color: Sophisticated (often dark or jewel tones)
- Examples: Gown, formal suit, elegant cocktail dress

**Formality Mixing Rule:**
- All pieces must roughly align (±1 level acceptable)
- Example: Business casual top + casual jeans = ✅ acceptable
- Example: Formal gown + casual sneakers = ❌ clashing formality
- Heritage Minimalist: Strongly prefers formality consistency
- Trend-Forward: Sometimes embraces formality clash for intentional effect

### Proportion Break

**Strategic Proportion Breaks:**
- **Cropped tops**: Expose waist, shorten torso, elongate legs (best with high-waisted bottoms)
- **High-low hemlines**: Create movement, add interest, modern
- **Asymmetrical hemlines**: One side longer; creates dynamic, trendy
- **Oversized with belt**: Defines waist, adds shape back

**Why Proportion Breaks Work:**
- Prevent monotony of same line throughout
- Create visual interest and movement
- Can strategically flatter different body shapes
- Must feel intentional (accidental crop = sloppy, not stylish)

## 4. HERITAGE MINIMALIST APPROACH

### Core Principles
- **Quality over quantity**: One excellent piece beats five mediocre pieces
- **Timelessness over trends**: Will this work in 5+ years?
- **Versatility**: Does this piece pair with 80%+ of the wardrobe?
- **Investment mentality**: Cost-per-wear calculation
- **Foundation first**: Strong neutral base before adding anything

### Color Philosophy
- Neutral foundation (70%): Black, white, gray, navy, camel, cream
- Neutral pops (20%): Soft earth tones, jewel tones that work with neutrals
- Accent colors (10%): Strategic bold color, sparingly

### Fabric Standards
- Natural fibers preferred (cotton, wool, linen, silk, cashmere)
- Quality indicators: Thread count, weight, hand feel, durability
- Avoid: Fast fashion, poor finishing, low-quality synthetics
- Construction: Seams are straight, even, well-finished; no loose threads

### Silhouette Preference
- Classic cuts that have worked for decades
- Well-proportioned to body
- Flattering without being trendy
- Timeless examples: Straight-leg jeans, tailored blazer, simple tee, A-line skirt

### Styling Approach
- Less is more: Fewer pieces, each intentional
- Monochromatic or analogous combinations
- Accessorizing: Minimal, quality pieces (watch, simple jewelry)
- No pattern mixing; solids with texture instead
- Tucking/layering for structure and intentionality

### Red Flags for Minimalist Perspective
- Trend-driven pieces (oversized, fast fashion proportions)
- Bold colors with short shelf life
- Poor construction or low-quality materials
- Pieces that serve only one purpose
- Anything that feels "costume-y" or lacks sophistication

## 5. TREND-FORWARD APPROACH

### Core Principles
- **Self-expression**: Fashion is personal; wear what excites you
- **Fashion evolution**: Your style should grow; refresh seasonally
- **Bold choices**: Embrace color, pattern, and personality
- **Contemporary relevance**: What's happening in fashion now?
- **Personality as priority**: Does this feel authentically YOU?

### Color Philosophy
- Bold color foundation: Sage, rust, burgundy, jewel tones
- Contrasting accents: Pair bold with complementary colors
- Pattern mixing: Encouraged with intent
- No fear of "trendy" - that's the point
- Saturation matters: Muted = boring; vibrant = alive

### Fabric Standards
- Quality preferred, but trend-relevant
- Mix high and low (luxury piece + fast fashion accent)
- Interesting textures and fabrics (leather, satin, velvet, technical)
- Newness matters: Latest fabrics, finishes, innovations
- Construction: Good, but fashion-forwardness weighted equally

### Silhouette Preference
- Contemporary cuts that feel current
- Fashion-forward proportions (oversized, cropped, asymmetrical)
- Intentional styling through silhouette
- Pieces that show personality through design
- Examples: Oversized blazer, cropped pants, statement sleeves

### Styling Approach
- Bold color combinations: Complementary, unexpected pairings
- Pattern mixing: Different scales, unified through color
- Accessorizing: Statement pieces, multiple layers, intentional excess
- Tucking/cropping/knotting to create shape and movement
- Details matter: Unexpected pairings create impact

### Red Flags for Trend-Forward Perspective
- Safe, neutral choices that fade into background
- Overly classic cuts (feels costume-y, dated)
- Poor color saturation (muted = unconfident)
- Lack of personality or individuality
- Too "matchy" (boring, uninspired matching)
- Anything that feels like it's trying too hard (vs. effortlessly intentional)

## 6. EVALUATION CHECKLIST

### For Single Items:

**Heritage Minimalist Evaluator:**
- ☐ Quality of construction (seams, finishes, materials)
- ☐ Color versatility (pairs with how much of wardrobe?)
- ☐ Silhouette timelessness (will this work in 5+ years?)
- ☐ Proportion to body (flattering, proportionate?)
- ☐ Investment value (cost-per-wear reasonable?)
- ☐ Formality level clarity (serves clear purpose)

**Trend-Forward Evaluator:**
- ☐ Color boldness and saturation (confident, vibrant?)
- ☐ Style expression (reflects personal taste, unique?)
- ☐ Fashion relevance (contemporary, on-trend?)
- ☐ Personal impact (elevates confidence/presence?)
- ☐ Design intentionality (creative choices, personality?)
- ☐ Proportion excitement (flatters AND interesting?)

### For Complete Outfits:

**Heritage Minimalist Evaluator:**
- ☐ Color harmony (timeless, versatile palette?)
- ☐ Formality consistency (all pieces aligned?)
- ☐ Silhouette balance (not all fitted OR all loose?)
- ☐ Quality cohesion (all pieces well-made?)
- ☐ Proportion balance (visual weight distributed?)
- ☐ Occasion clarity (serves clear purpose?)
- ☐ Future-proof (will work in 5+ years?)

**Trend-Forward Evaluator:**
- ☐ Color boldness (confident, contemporary palette?)
- ☐ Style cohesion (pieces "speak same language"?)
- ☐ Silhouette interest (proportions create movement?)
- ☐ Personal expression (outfit reflects wearer's style?)
- ☐ Fashion relevance (contemporary, trendy choices?)
- ☐ Intentional styling (looks curated, not accidental?)
- ☐ Personality impact (outfit elevates presence?)

## 7. COMMON STYLING MISTAKES

### Both Personas Recognize These Issues:

**Poor Proportion Balance**
- All fitted pieces (claustrophobic, revealing)
- All loose pieces (shapeless, overwhelming)
- Cropped top without high waist (disproportionate)
- Oversized without anchoring detail (drowning)

**Formality Mismatch**
- Formal gown + casual sneakers
- Business blazer + beachwear
- Black-tie top + casual bottom

**Silhouette Confusion**
- Multiple competing silhouettes (no clear focal point)
- Clashing textures without intention (rough + shiny + smooth = busy)
- Sleeve/hem lengths creating awkward proportions

**Fit Issues**
- Shoulder seams not at shoulder point
- Pants/skirt sitting at widest part of hip (unflattering)
- Sleeves ending mid-forearm awkwardly
- Shirt hitting at hip bulge (most unflattering point)

**Color Issues (Persona-Dependent)**
- Minimalist: Too many competing colors (visual chaos)
- Trend-Forward: Too many colors without color bridge (clashing)
- Either: Poor color value contrast (light on light, dark on dark = can't see silhouette)

**Visual Weight Crashes**
- All heavy elements (dark, busy, thick) in one area = weighed down
- Light pieces swallowing frame = overwhelmed
- Heavy weight at bottom, light at top = unstable feeling
